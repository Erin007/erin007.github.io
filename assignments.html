Week 1: Calculator

.

We wrote calculator programs that could add, multiply, subtract and divide two numbers. Okay, so it's no T1-89, but it sure felt like a jump from the "Hello World" program I had only recently mastered. This project was the first where I...

.

  • dealt with error handling for user input (albeit in a rudimentary way)
  • refactored a long, long list of if statements into a more readable case statement
  • used an unless loop to let a user continue "playing"

.

After meeting the baseline requirements, I wrote a little code that found factors of numbers, including negatives and identified prime numbers.

Week 2: Solar System

.

This week was a meteor shower of new information. We worked on a planetary project wherein we created objects in a planet class that have a variety of attributes. We completed the project in two waves...

.

  • Wave 1: was the first time I used classes, and initialized new objects
  • Wave 2: stepped it up a notch by asking us to use an array of planets to initialize a solar system object of a new class

    • .

    I also tackled some optional enhancements such as calculating the distance between two planets using their distance from the sun attributes. It was out of this world! Through completing this project, I finally understand why everyone keeps harping on the idea that "everything is an object" in Ruby.

Week 3: Bank Accounts

.

Using a Bank module, we created various classes for different aspects of bank accounts. The assignment started out straightforaward enough, until objects of different classes had to associate with one another based on shared attributes. Whoa. Takeaways:

.

  • Class inheritance saves you from repetition. It's super!
  • I figured out how to raise Exceptions with useful messages. Don't even think about opening that Money Market account with less than $10,000.
  • sprintf "%.2f" is a beautiful little snippet of code

    • .

    You can take a peak at the final code here, and see my first ever class methods and read of info from a csv. Adios, gets.chomp. You can also see the comment relics of my first great "end" scavenger hunt. I'm totally ready to make secure accounts for a real bank, right?

Week 5: Farmers' Markets

.

The previous cohorts warned us it was coming - the elusive "Far Mar". OMG get ready for CSVs and TDD! Realistically, the assignment was managable. It was an opportunity to put together all of the individual Ruby concepts we'd studied. Here's my advice for future FarMar folks:

.

  • Make a diagram ahead of time for how to you expect your classes to interact with one another and make sure you're crystal clear about what type of object a method should return.
  • Make some tea while you wait. Reading in from a CSV with thousands of lines is a time intensive process. If you don't optimize it, your tests take forever.
  • Make your tests print out something every time a loop runs to let you know if your test is actually running. Emojis work best.

    • .

    My whole FarMar project could use some refactoring. If the goal is DRY code, there was a rainstorm at my Farmer's Market. But, alas, it has the baseline functionality and a slew of optional requirements.

Weeks 11 & 12: Petsy

.

Petsy was a beast of a project, and not just because we chose to make ours about animals. As a group of 3 people, we spent the last two weeks making a mock e-commerce site loosely based on Etsy. This was the largest project with the most ambiguous guidelines so far, and we had to tackle deeply philosophical questions:

.

  • What is a cart? What type of users can have a cart? If there are different types of users, are they different models or the same model with different attributes? How can a cart and its contents persist across sessions? What is a cookie?
  • How do you resolve a merge conflict? Can we still be friends if all of the validations I just wrote broke everything you've worked on for three days? What is a join table? How do I use Trello? Does KanBan know?
  • How can users log in securely via a third party site? How magical is Rails? Will we actaully finish this project?

.

The final site is pretty snazzy and even features a Ryan Gosling cameo. Plus, it's chockfull of groan-worthy animal puns - always an important feature.
